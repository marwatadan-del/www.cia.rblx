The Agency

Who We Are

The CIA is a civilian foreign intelligence service of the federal government of the United States, tasked with gathering, processing, and analyzing national security information from around the world. As a principal member of the United States Intelligence Community (IC), we report to the Director of National Intelligence.

Unlike the FBI, which is a domestic security service, the CIA has no law enforcement function and is primarily focused on overseas intelligence gathering. We work alongside partner agencies across the IC and with allied services worldwide.

The CIA operates from its headquarters in Langley, Virginia, with officers stationed worldwide. Our work spans collection, analysis, counterintelligence, covert action, and the research and development of technical capabilities.

Overview

About the Central Intelligence Agency

The Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) is an independent civilian intelligence agency of the United States federal government. It is one of the principal members of the United States Intelligence Community (IC) and is under the direction of the Director of National Intelligence. The CIA's primary mission is to collect, analyze, evaluate, and disseminate foreign intelligence to assist the President and senior U.S. government policymakers in making decisions relating to national security.

The CIA additionally serves as the national manager for human source intelligence (HUMINT) and coordinates HUMINT activities across the IC. It also conducts covert action at the direction of the President. The CIA does not make policy; it is an agency that provides intelligence and analysis to those who do.

Unlike the FBI, which is a domestic security service, the CIA has no law enforcement function and is mainly focused on intelligence gathering overseas. Its collection activities are focused on foreign governments, corporations, and individuals. Information is gathered through human intelligence (HUMINT), signals intelligence (SIGINT), imagery intelligence (IMINT), and open-source intelligence (OSINT).

The CIA is headquartered at the George Bush Center for Intelligence in Langley, Virginia, a few miles west of Washington, D.C. Its employees are subject to security clearance requirements, and many work under classified programs. The Agency maintains offices and stations throughout the world.

At a Glance

Founded

September 18, 1947, by the National Security Act of 1947. Predecessor organization: the Office of Strategic Services (OSS), established 1942.

Headquarters

George Bush Center for Intelligence, Langley (McLean), Virginia, United States.

Budget

Classified. Part of the National Intelligence Program (NIP). The aggregate intelligence budget was approximately $90 billion (FY2023, ODNI disclosed figure).

Workforce

Exact number classified. Publicly estimated at 21,000–27,000 employees across all functions worldwide.

Parent Organization

Office of the Director of National Intelligence (ODNI). The CIA Director reports to the President through the DNI and the National Security Council.

Organizational Reporting

Chain of Command

Level I

The President

The CIA's ultimate authority. The President receives the President's Daily Brief (PDB), directs covert action through signed Findings, and appoints the Director of Central Intelligence.

Level II

National Security Council

The principal forum for senior-level national security policy deliberation. The NSC coordinates the CIA's role in implementing national security objectives and reviews covert action programs.

Level III

Director of National Intelligence

Oversees the entire 17-member Intelligence Community. The CIA Director reports to the DNI and is a member of the National Security Council.

Mission Statement

Our Mission

"We are the nation's first line of defense. We accomplish what others cannot accomplish and go where others cannot go."

The CIA's mission is to preempt threats and further U.S. national security objectives by collecting intelligence that matters, producing objective all-source analysis, conducting effective covert action as directed by the President, and safeguarding the secrets that help keep our nation safe.

This mission is carried out by dedicated men and women who serve with exceptional courage, integrity, and professionalism. The CIA exists to provide the President and senior national security policymakers with the information they need to make sound decisions about U.S. foreign policy, national security, and military operations.

Mission Objectives
  • Collect intelligence through human sources and other appropriate means, with collection focused on adversaries, failing states, and threats to U.S. interests abroad.
  • Correlate, evaluate, and disseminate intelligence from all sources and conduct all-source analysis to produce finished intelligence for the President and senior policymakers.
  • Provide overall direction for and coordination of the collection of national intelligence outside the United States through human sources by elements of the Intelligence Community.
  • Conduct counterintelligence activities outside the United States and, in coordination with other IC members, conduct counterintelligence activities within the United States.
  • Conduct covert action activities approved by the President and coordinate covert action activities of other agencies, when authorized by the President.
  • Perform such other functions and duties related to intelligence affecting the national security as the President or the Director of National Intelligence may direct.
Vision Statement

Our Vision

"An Agency that makes a difference by delivering superior intelligence and covert capabilities to those who need them, when they need them."

The CIA envisions a world in which the United States is better protected from threats, better informed about global developments, and better positioned to advance its national interests through the effective use of intelligence.

Our vision encompasses not only what we do for the nation, but how we do it — with integrity, objectivity, and service. The Agency aspires to be a model organization: one that attracts and retains the best and brightest talent, invests in innovation, and operates with the highest ethical standards.

Strategic Priorities

Strategic Competition

Counter the strategic challenges posed by China and Russia across all domains — political, economic, military, technological, and informational.

Counterterrorism

Disrupt, degrade, and defeat terrorist organizations that threaten the U.S. homeland and American interests abroad.

Counterproliferation

Prevent the spread of weapons of mass destruction and the delivery systems that could threaten the U.S. and its allies.

Digital Modernization

Leverage emerging technologies — artificial intelligence, cloud computing, and advanced analytics — to gain decisive intelligence advantages.

Our Principles

What We Stand For

These values are not aspirational — they are the lived reality of what it means to serve as a CIA officer. They guide decisions made in the field, in the analysis center, in the laboratory, and in every interaction with our partners, the public, and each other.

I

Integrity

We adhere to the highest standards of lawful conduct. We are truthful and forthright. We honor the public trust placed in us and resist arrogance. We acknowledge our mistakes and learn from them. We never misrepresent facts to policymakers, even when the truth is unwelcome.

II

Excellence

We hold ourselves to the highest standards and demand the same from each other. We provide superior intelligence support, continuously improving our methods and products. We invest in our people, recognizing that excellence begins with talented, well-trained officers who are given the tools they need to succeed.

III

Courage

We face hard truths and difficult challenges with confidence and resolve. Physical courage under dangerous conditions and moral courage to speak truth to power — both define the CIA officer. We make difficult decisions in the face of danger, always acting in the interest of our country and its people.

IV

Objectivity

We seek and speak the truth unvarnished and without bias. Our assessments are based on the weight of evidence and sound analytical judgment, not on what policymakers want to hear. We clearly distinguish between intelligence and policy, never allowing our analysis to be shaped by political considerations.

V

Service

We dedicate ourselves to the mission — protecting America's security and supporting those who make critical national security decisions. Our work is a calling, not merely a career. We serve with a sense of purpose that transcends personal ambition, understanding that the stakes of our work are extraordinarily high.

VI

Teamwork

We work as one team, recognizing that our collective effort is greater than the sum of our individual contributions. We collaborate across the Intelligence Community and with international partners. We share information appropriately, subordinate individual interests to mission success, and support each other through the most challenging assignments.

The Ethos of Service

What It Means to Serve

To serve in the CIA is to embrace a life of extraordinary commitment. Our officers work in conditions of secrecy, often unable to share the details of their contributions with family and friends. They deploy to some of the most dangerous regions in the world. They make decisions that affect national security with incomplete information and under intense time pressure.

Yet CIA officers also experience the profound satisfaction of knowing their work matters — that the intelligence they collect, the analysis they produce, and the operations they conduct make a tangible difference in protecting the nation and its allies.

The CIA Memorial Wall, inscribed with stars representing officers who gave their lives in service, stands as a solemn testament to the ultimate sacrifice that some have made. As of 2024, 140 stars are carved into the marble wall — each one representing a fallen officer whose name, in many cases, remains classified.

The Memorial Wall

Located in the main lobby of the Original Headquarters Building, the CIA Memorial Wall honors those who gave their lives in service to their country. Stars are carved into the white Alabama marble — one for each employee who died in the line of duty.

The Book of Honor, displayed below the wall, contains the names of those whose identities can be publicly acknowledged. Many entries remain blank — the officer's name classified even in death.

Executive Branch Oversight

How the CIA is Overseen

The CIA operates under a robust system of oversight involving all three branches of government. This system of checks and balances ensures that the Agency fulfills its mission within the bounds of U.S. law, democratic values, and constitutional principles.

The President provides ultimate direction to the CIA through the National Security Council, executive orders, and presidential findings that authorize covert action. The Director of National Intelligence oversees the CIA within the broader Intelligence Community framework established by the Intelligence Reform and Terrorism Prevention Act of 2004.

National Security Council (NSC)

Reviews and approves covert action programs. The NSC Principals and Deputies Committees provide policy-level oversight of CIA activities.

President's Intelligence Advisory Board (PIAB)

An independent executive branch body that advises the President on the quality, quantity, and adequacy of intelligence collection and analysis.

Office of the DNI Inspector General

Provides IC-wide oversight of intelligence programs and conducts reviews that cross agency jurisdictions.

Congressional Oversight

Legislative Controls

Congressional oversight is a cornerstone of democratic accountability for the CIA. Two dedicated committees — the Senate Select Committee on Intelligence (SSCI) and the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence (HPSCI) — provide robust oversight of all intelligence activities.

The CIA Director and other senior officials regularly testify before these committees, both in open and closed sessions. The Agency provides classified briefings on sensitive programs and operations as required by law and tradition.

Senate Select Committee on Intelligence (SSCI)

Provides oversight of the CIA's budget, programs, and activities. Reviews and authorizes intelligence authorization bills. Receives notifications of covert action findings.

House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence (HPSCI)

Exercises jurisdiction over the CIA in the House of Representatives. Shares oversight responsibilities with SSCI on the annual intelligence authorization bill.

Appropriations Subcommittees

The Defense Appropriations subcommittees in both chambers control the CIA's budget appropriations, providing a second layer of congressional financial oversight.

Intelligence Disciplines

Collection Methods

The CIA collects intelligence using a range of methods and disciplines. Each collection method has unique strengths and limitations, and experienced intelligence officers integrate multiple collection streams to produce comprehensive assessments.

HUMINT

Human Intelligence

The oldest form of intelligence collection. CIA case officers recruit and handle human sources — individuals with access to information about adversary governments, terrorist organizations, weapons programs, and other matters of national security concern. HUMINT provides unique access to intentions and plans that technical collection cannot reach.

SIGINT

Signals Intelligence

Collection of intelligence from electronic signals and systems — including communications intelligence (COMINT), electronic intelligence (ELINT), and foreign instrumentation signals intelligence (FISINT). Coordinated with the National Security Agency, which serves as the national manager for SIGINT.

GEOINT

Geospatial Intelligence

Analysis and exploitation of imagery and geospatial information. The CIA works with the National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA) to exploit satellite and aerial imagery for intelligence production, military support, and threat assessment.

OSINT

Open-Source Intelligence

Collection and analysis of publicly available information — news media, academic publications, social media, and government data. The CIA's Open Source Enterprise (OSE) manages OSINT activities and produces the World Factbook, one of the most widely used reference publications in the world.

CYBINT

Cyber Intelligence

Collection of intelligence from and about cyberspace, including the activities of foreign government hackers, criminal networks, and terrorist groups operating online. The Directorate of Digital Innovation leads CIA efforts in this domain.

MASINT

Measurement & Signature Intelligence

Collection of intelligence using specialized technical sensors that measure and record physical phenomena — including nuclear, chemical, biological, optical, acoustic, and radiofrequency signatures — to characterize targets and detect threats.

Finished Intelligence

Intelligence Products

The CIA produces a range of finished intelligence products tailored to the needs of different consumers — from the President to military commanders to foreign policy analysts.

Daily

President's Daily Brief

The PDB is the most sensitive intelligence document in the U.S. government. Produced daily by CIA analysts and delivered to the President and a small number of senior officials, it covers the most urgent and significant intelligence developments worldwide.

Weekly

National Intelligence Digest

A broader compilation of current intelligence provided to senior policymakers across the national security community. The NID covers a wider range of topics and reaches a larger audience than the PDB.

Periodic

National Intelligence Estimates

NIEs are the IC's most authoritative written judgments on national security topics. Coordinated across all IC members, they represent the community's best assessment of a particular issue and are designed to inform long-term policy decisions.

Operational

Tactical Intelligence Reports

Time-sensitive intelligence products that support ongoing military and intelligence operations. These include warning intelligence, targeting support, and operational assessments tailored to the needs of combatant commanders.

Research

Intelligence Assessments

In-depth analytical papers that examine a particular topic in depth. These papers support longer-term policy planning and provide context for current developments.

Reference

World Factbook & Atlas

The CIA's flagship open-source reference publication, providing detailed information on the history, geography, government, economy, and people of 266 world entities. Available to the public online.

The Five Directorates

How We're Organized

The CIA is organized into five directorates and several mission centers. Each directorate is led by a director who reports to the CIA Director. Mission centers cut across directorate boundaries to integrate collection, analysis, and operations against specific threats.

DO

Directorate of Operations

The National Clandestine Service — the human intelligence arm of the CIA. Case officers build and manage networks of sources in foreign countries, collecting intelligence on the intentions and capabilities of adversaries. The DO also conducts covert action programs authorized by the President. Stations and bases around the world are managed by the DO.

DA

Directorate of Analysis

The all-source analytical workforce that produces finished intelligence for senior policymakers. DA analysts cover every region of the world and every major functional issue — from counterterrorism to economic intelligence to weapons of mass destruction. The DA prepares the President's Daily Brief and coordinates National Intelligence Estimates across the IC.

DS&T

Directorate of Science & Technology

Engineers, scientists, and technologists who develop the collection systems and analytical tools that give the U.S. intelligence advantages over adversaries. The DS&T created some of the most significant technical intelligence systems in history, including the U-2 reconnaissance aircraft and early satellite imagery systems. Today it focuses on advanced sensors, data analytics, and emerging technologies.

DS

Directorate of Support

The administrative backbone of the CIA — providing logistics, security, finance, human resources, medical support, facilities management, and all other functions that enable the Agency's mission. Support officers are embedded in CIA stations around the world, providing essential services to officers operating in challenging environments.

DDI

Directorate of Digital Innovation

Established in 2015, the DDI is the newest of the CIA's directorates. It accelerates the integration of cutting-edge digital and cyber capabilities across all CIA activities. The DDI is responsible for IT infrastructure, cybersecurity, data analytics, cloud computing, and the application of artificial intelligence to intelligence challenges.

MC

Mission Centers

Mission Centers integrate collection, analysis, and operations against specific threats or geographic areas, cutting across directorate boundaries. Major Mission Centers include the Counterterrorism Center (CTC), the Counterproliferation Center (CPC), the Counterintelligence Mission Center (CIMC), and regional mission centers covering major geographic areas of interest.

The Intelligence Cycle

How raw information becomes finished intelligence that informs national security decisions.

Six Phases

From Collection to Decision

The intelligence cycle is the process by which information is acquired, converted into intelligence, and made available to policymakers. It is an ongoing, iterative process — the output of one cycle becomes an input for the next.

01
Phase I

Planning & Direction

Senior policymakers identify intelligence needs. The National Intelligence Priorities Framework (NIPF), approved by the President, guides what intelligence is collected. The CIA Director works with the DNI to translate policy needs into collection and analytical requirements.

02
Phase II

Collection

Intelligence is gathered from human sources, technical collection systems (SIGINT, GEOINT, MASINT), and open sources. The CIA coordinates human intelligence collection globally and manages collection from unique technical systems developed by the DS&T.

03
Phase III

Processing

Raw data is converted into usable form. This includes translation of foreign-language materials, decryption of intercepted communications, processing of imagery, and conversion of technical sensor data into interpretable formats. Much of this work is automated using advanced analytical tools.

04
Phase IV

All-Source Analysis

Analysts from the DA integrate information from all collection disciplines to assess its significance, evaluate its reliability, and integrate it into a coherent picture. Analysts apply structured analytical techniques to minimize cognitive biases and produce objective assessments.

05
Phase V

Dissemination

Finished intelligence is delivered to policymakers, military commanders, and other consumers through classified publications, briefings, and secure electronic systems. The PDB is delivered to the President daily; other products reach a broader senior-level audience.

06
Phase VI

Feedback & Evaluation

Consumers evaluate intelligence products and provide feedback to guide future collection and analysis. This feedback loop allows the Intelligence Community to continuously refine its priorities, improve analytical tradecraft, and better serve the needs of policymakers.

Institutional Memory

History of the Agency

Seven decades of service to the nation, from the Cold War to the digital age.

From OSS to CIA

Origins of American Intelligence

American foreign intelligence traces its origins to the Revolutionary War, when General George Washington employed a network of spies to gather information on British forces. However, the United States did not establish a permanent peacetime intelligence agency until after World War II.

During World War II, President Franklin D. Roosevelt established the Office of Strategic Services (OSS) in 1942 under General William "Wild Bill" Donovan. The OSS conducted espionage, covert operations, and propaganda behind enemy lines in Europe and Asia. Though dissolved at war's end, the OSS laid the institutional and cultural foundations for the CIA.

The National Security Act of 1947, signed by President Harry S. Truman on September 18, 1947, formally established the Central Intelligence Agency. The Act created a unified national security apparatus — including the CIA, the National Security Council, and the position of Secretary of Defense — to coordinate the nation's response to the emerging Cold War threat.

Predecessor

Office of Strategic Services (OSS)

Established 1942 by President Roosevelt under Gen. William Donovan. Conducted espionage, sabotage, and special operations in World War II. Dissolved October 1, 1945. Many OSS veterans formed the nucleus of the CIA.

Transition

Central Intelligence Group (CIG)

Interim organization established by President Truman in January 1946 to coordinate intelligence activities pending permanent legislation. The CIG was folded into the CIA upon the Agency's founding in September 1947.

1947

National Security Act — CIA Founded

President Truman signs the National Security Act on September 18, formally establishing the CIA. Rear Admiral Roscoe Hillenkoetter is appointed as the first Director of Central Intelligence.

1950

Korean War Intelligence

The CIA faces its first major operational test as North Korea invades South Korea. The Agency expands rapidly, developing its analytical and clandestine capabilities. The war exposes early weaknesses in collection and warning intelligence.

1953

Operation AJAX / BOOT

Working with British intelligence (MI6), the CIA conducts Operation AJAX to restore Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi to the Iranian throne after Prime Minister Mohammad Mosaddegh nationalizes the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company. The operation succeeds but leaves a legacy of anti-American sentiment.

1956

U-2 Overflights of the USSR Begin

The CIA's U-2 reconnaissance aircraft begins overflights of the Soviet Union, providing unprecedented imagery intelligence on Soviet military capabilities. When Francis Gary Powers is shot down on May 1, 1960, the incident creates a major diplomatic crisis with the USSR.

1961

Bay of Pigs Invasion

A CIA-trained paramilitary force of Cuban exiles (Brigade 2506) attempts to invade Cuba and overthrow Fidel Castro at the Bay of Pigs. The operation fails catastrophically. President Kennedy publicly takes responsibility. An internal IG investigation reveals profound planning and execution failures.

1962

Cuban Missile Crisis

CIA imagery analysts identify Soviet ballistic missiles in Cuba on October 14, 1962. President Kennedy is briefed on the discovery, triggering a 13-day standoff with the Soviet Union. CIA intelligence is central to the resolution of the most dangerous nuclear confrontation in history.

1975

Church & Pike Committee Investigations

Congressional investigations led by Sen. Frank Church and Rep. Otis Pike expose CIA activities including assassination plots against foreign leaders, domestic surveillance programs (COINTELPRO, Operation CHAOS), and MKULTRA human experimentation. The resulting reforms include the Hughes-Ryan Amendment, requiring presidential findings for covert action, and the establishment of permanent intelligence oversight committees.

1986

Iran-Contra Affair

A covert program to sell arms to Iran and divert proceeds to Nicaraguan Contra rebels is exposed. CIA Director William Casey dies before he can be called to testify. The affair triggers further reforms to covert action oversight and congressional notification requirements.

1994

Aldrich Ames Arrested

CIA officer Aldrich Ames is arrested on espionage charges. Over nearly a decade, Ames had provided the KGB with the identities of virtually every CIA asset in the Soviet Union, leading to the death or imprisonment of ten intelligence assets. The case triggers a sweeping review of CIA security practices.

2001

September 11 & Global War on Terror

CIA officers are on the ground in Afghanistan within weeks of the September 11 attacks, working with Northern Alliance forces and supporting the initial military campaign. The post-9/11 era transforms the CIA, with massive expansion of counterterrorism programs, enhanced interrogation (later discontinued), and the establishment of the Counterterrorism Center as the Agency's largest operational entity.

2004

Intelligence Reform Act

The Intelligence Reform and Terrorism Prevention Act creates the Director of National Intelligence, shifting some authorities away from the CIA Director. The CIA reorganizes, creating new mission centers to better integrate collection and analysis.

2011

Operation Neptune Spear

Years of CIA intelligence work, including analysis of communications patterns and courier networks, lead to the identification of Osama bin Laden's compound in Abbottabad, Pakistan. On May 2, 2011, U.S. Navy SEALs conduct the raid that kills bin Laden, ending a decade-long manhunt.

2015

Directorate of Digital Innovation Established

CIA Director John Brennan establishes the Directorate of Digital Innovation — the CIA's first new directorate in 52 years. The DDI signals the Agency's commitment to integrating digital technologies, big data analytics, and cyber capabilities across all mission areas.

Overview

Why Work at the CIA

The CIA employs people from virtually every professional background — united by a commitment to serving their country and a tolerance for working in conditions of extraordinary secrecy. Whether your expertise is in languages, engineering, finance, law, medicine, psychology, or data science, there is likely a role at the CIA where your skills can contribute to the nation's security.

CIA employees work at our headquarters in Langley, Virginia, at facilities across the United States, and at overseas stations and bases. Many positions require significant overseas deployment. All positions require a Top Secret/SCI security clearance.

The application process is thorough — typically six months to a year — including a background investigation, polygraph examination, medical evaluation, and psychological assessment. The rigor reflects the trust the Agency places in its employees and the sensitivity of the information they handle.

Requirements

U.S. Citizenship

All applicants must be U.S. citizens. Dual citizenship is evaluated on a case-by-case basis and may disqualify candidates in certain roles.

Security Clearance

All employees must obtain and maintain a Top Secret/SCI security clearance. Prior drug use, financial problems, and foreign contacts may affect eligibility.

Polygraph Examination

All new employees undergo a polygraph examination as part of the pre-employment process. Continued employment requires periodic reinvestigation.

Medical Evaluation

A comprehensive medical evaluation is required. Standards vary by position; overseas and operational positions have more stringent requirements.

Benefits

Compensation & Benefits

Competitive Salary

CIA salaries are competitive with the private sector and follow the GS pay scale for most positions, with additional pay for overseas service and hazardous duty.

Health & Dental

Comprehensive Federal Employees Health Benefits (FEHB) plans covering medical, dental, and vision, with Agency contributions to premiums.

Retirement

Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS) with Agency matching for the Thrift Savings Plan (TSP), equivalent to a 401(k).

Work-Life Balance

Generous leave policies, flexible work arrangements where mission permits, childcare subsidies, and wellness programs.

TRACK // 01 · OPERATIONS

Clandestine Service Officer

The heart of the CIA's human intelligence mission. Operations Officers are trained in tradecraft, foreign languages, and interpersonal skills. They build and manage relationships with foreign nationals who provide intelligence on matters of national security concern. Extensive overseas deployment required.

  • Foreign language proficiency strongly preferred
  • Bachelor's degree minimum; advanced degree beneficial
  • Extensive overseas travel and deployment
  • Exceptional interpersonal and communication skills
  • Ability to work under cover and maintain a cover identity
  • Physical fitness and adaptability
TRACK // 02 · ANALYSIS

Intelligence Analyst

All-source analysts synthesize information from multiple collection disciplines to produce finished intelligence for the President, Cabinet, and senior policymakers. Analysts develop deep expertise in a region, country, or functional issue — from counterterrorism to economics to weapons programs.

  • Bachelor's or advanced degree in relevant field
  • Strong research and writing skills
  • Regional, functional, or technical expertise
  • Foreign language proficiency a plus
  • Critical thinking and structured analytical techniques
  • Ability to communicate complex issues clearly
TRACK // 03 · SCIENCE & TECHNOLOGY

Engineer / Scientist

Engineers and scientists in the Directorate of Science & Technology develop the collection systems, sensors, processing tools, and analytical capabilities that provide intelligence advantages. Disciplines include aerospace engineering, electrical engineering, computer science, physics, chemistry, and biology.

  • Bachelor's degree in STEM required; advanced degree preferred
  • Relevant technical expertise and research experience
  • Ability to apply engineering to intelligence problems
  • Security engineering and systems integration experience beneficial
  • Innovative thinking and problem-solving orientation
TRACK // 04 · DIGITAL INNOVATION

Data Scientist / Cyber Officer

Data scientists, software engineers, cybersecurity specialists, and AI/ML practitioners in the Directorate of Digital Innovation develop and deploy cutting-edge digital capabilities to collect intelligence, protect CIA systems, and exploit adversary networks.

  • Computer science, data science, or cybersecurity background
  • Experience with machine learning, AI, or advanced analytics
  • Software development and systems engineering skills
  • Cybersecurity certifications or experience beneficial
  • Cloud computing and big data experience valued
TRACK // 05 · LANGUAGE

Foreign Language Officer

The CIA needs officers with proficiency in critical foreign languages — Arabic, Mandarin Chinese, Farsi, Russian, Korean, Pashto, Dari, Urdu, and others. Language officers work as translators, interpreters, and intelligence officers leveraging their linguistic skills in collection and analysis.

  • Native or near-native proficiency in a critical language
  • U.S. citizenship (foreign-born applicants evaluated carefully)
  • Cultural understanding and area expertise
  • Translation and interpretation skills
  • Intelligence analysis skills highly valued
TRACK // 06 · SUPPORT

Mission Support Officer

Mission support officers work in finance, human resources, logistics, security, facilities, legal, and other enabling functions. They ensure CIA operations run effectively at headquarters and in overseas stations.

  • Professional degree or experience in relevant field
  • CPA, legal, HR, or logistics background beneficial
  • Strong organizational and administrative skills
  • Ability to work in overseas or challenging environments
  • Customer service orientation and team focus
TRACK // 07 · MEDICAL

Medical Officer / Psychologist

Physicians, nurses, psychologists, and other healthcare professionals provide medical services to CIA employees worldwide. Medical officers deploy to overseas stations, assess the physical and psychological readiness of employees, and support special operations.

  • Medical degree (MD or DO) or advanced nursing/psychology degree
  • Board certification in relevant specialty
  • Willingness to serve in austere or hazardous environments
  • Experience with occupational or travel medicine valued
  • Psychological assessment experience for psychologists
TRACK // 08 · LEADERSHIP

Executive Development Program

The CIA's executive development programs identify and nurture future leaders across all career tracks. Selected mid-career employees receive leadership training, interagency rotations, and targeted assignments to prepare them for senior positions within the Agency.

  • Strong performance record in current track
  • Leadership potential and executive presence
  • Broad understanding of CIA mission and IC
  • Demonstrated ability to lead teams and projects
  • Cross-functional experience valued

Application Process

How to Apply

Step 1

Online Application

Submit your resume, transcripts, and application materials through the CIA's secure online portal. Ensure all information is accurate — inconsistencies may trigger disqualification.

Step 2

Initial Review

Recruiters review applications for minimum qualifications, clearance eligibility, and mission fit. Selected candidates are contacted for interviews, typically 4–8 weeks after application.

Step 3

Interviews & Testing

Multiple rounds of interviews, including cognitive and personality assessments. Technical candidates may complete skills tests. Language candidates are tested for proficiency.

A thorough investigation of your personal history, including finances, foreign contacts, travel, and lifestyle. The polygraph examination is a key component of this phase.

FAQs

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I tell my family I work for the CIA?
Yes. CIA employees may acknowledge their employment with the Agency to immediate family and close friends. However, they may not disclose the specific nature of their work, classified programs they support, or the identities of colleagues.
How long does the security clearance process take?
The process typically takes six months to a year from application to employment. The timeline depends on the complexity of your background, responsiveness of references, and investigative workload. International travel, foreign contacts, and dual citizenship can add time.
Does prior marijuana use disqualify me?
Not necessarily. The CIA considers drug use on a case-by-case basis, looking at recency, frequency, and circumstances. Applicants must be honest about prior use. Recent or heavy use is more likely to be disqualifying than occasional use in the distant past.
Can I have a foreign spouse or partner?
Foreign national spouses, partners, or family members are evaluated as part of the security clearance process. Depending on the nationality and circumstances, a foreign-national spouse may affect clearance eligibility for certain positions.
Will I need to move to the Washington, D.C. area?
Most CIA positions are based at headquarters in Langley, Virginia (McLean, VA, near Washington D.C.). Some positions are located at other domestic facilities. Operational and some support positions require overseas deployment.

About the Factbook

The World Factbook

The World Factbook has been produced annually since 1962. It provides basic intelligence on the history, people, government, economy, energy, geography, environment, communications, transportation, military, terrorism, and transnational issues for 266 world entities.

Originally classified, the Factbook was made publicly available in 1975 and is now one of the most widely-used public domain references in the world. It is used by schools, universities, businesses, governments, and international organizations around the globe.

The Factbook is updated continuously throughout the year, with information sourced from a wide range of open-source materials including official government statistics, international organizations, and published demographic data. All information in the Factbook is unclassified and available for public use without restriction.

The Freedom of Information Act

FOIA at the CIA

The Freedom of Information Act (FOIA), 5 U.S.C. § 552, provides the public with the right to request access to records from any federal agency. The CIA's Information Management Services (IMS) processes FOIA and Privacy Act requests for records held by the Agency.

The CIA processes FOIA requests in two queues: a Simple Track for straightforward requests expected to yield fewer than 500 pages, and a Complex Track for requests requiring more extensive searches, review, or classification determinations. Processing times vary widely depending on the complexity and sensitivity of the requested records.

Documents approved for release are added to the CREST (CIA Records Search Tool) database, which provides online access to more than 13 million pages of released CIA records. CREST is searchable by keyword, document date, and document type.

How to Submit a Request

Step 1: Define Your Request

Identify the specific records you are requesting. Narrow, specific requests are processed faster than broad requests. Providing dates, names, and document types helps the Agency conduct an efficient search.

Step 2: Submit in Writing

Submit your request in writing to the Information Management Services, CIA, Washington, D.C. 20505, or electronically through the CIA's online FOIA portal. Include your full name, address, and a detailed description of the requested records.

Step 3: Await Acknowledgment

You will receive a written acknowledgment with a case number. Retain this number for tracking purposes. The CIA will notify you of any fees associated with processing your request.

Step 4: Receive a Determination

The CIA will provide a written determination granting or denying your request, in full or in part. Denied requests will cite the applicable FOIA exemptions. You may appeal denials to the Agency Release Panel.

FOIA Exemptions

Applicable Exemptions

The CIA routinely invokes the following FOIA exemptions when withholding information:

  • Exemption 1: Classified national security information
  • Exemption 3: Information protected by statute (including the CIA Act of 1949 and the National Security Act)
  • Exemption 5: Deliberative process privilege and attorney-client communications
  • Exemption 6: Personal privacy information
  • Exemption 7(C): Law enforcement records that could constitute an invasion of personal privacy

CREST Database

The CIA Records Search Tool (CREST) provides online access to declassified CIA records. The database contains over 13 million pages of documents, including historical intelligence assessments, operational cables, and administrative records.
